19.1 Experiment 1 - Volunteer’s Dilemma

This game is discussed in detail in Chapter 17 of the text.

If you find it interesting, here is some further reading:

Goeree, Jacob K., Charles A. Holt, and Angela M. Smith. “An experimental examination of the volunteer’s dilemma.” Games and Economic Behavior 102 (2017): 303-315.

Bland, James R. “How many games are we playing? An experimental analysis of choice bracketing in games.” Journal of Behavioral and Experimental Economics 80 (2019): 80-91.

19.1.1 Instructions

Part 1 – Rounds 1-20

Rounds and Matchings: The experiment sets up two-person groups which interact in a sequence of rounds. Note: In each round, you will be matched with another person selected at random from the other participants. There will be a new random rematching each round.

Interdependence: The decisions that you and the other person make will determine your earnings.

Decisions: Both you and the other person will decide whether to incur a cost to undertake an action that may benefit both of you. You cannot see the other’s decision while choosing yours, and vice versa.

Duplication: The full benefit from this action is available to both of you if at least one person undertakes the costly action, and no additional benefit is accrued if both of you incur this cost.

Player Costs: The cost of the action will be the same for all people in each group.

Investment Cost: In each period or “round”, you will decide whether to make a costly decision, which we will refer to as an investment. If you decide to invest, you incur a cost of $0.20 in the current round. If your decision is not invest, you incur no cost.

Investment Benefit: If at least one person in your group decides to invest, both people in the group will receive an amount, $1.00, whether or not they invested in that round.

Earnings: If at least one person invests, then a person who invests earns $1.00 - their cost, and a person who does not invest earns $1.00. If neither person invests, they both earn $0.20.

Initial Capital: All of you start the first round with an earnings balance of $0.00. Earnings in each round will be added to this amount. The program will keep track of cumulative earnings for all rounds

In the following examples, please use the mouse button to select the best answer. Remember that the cost of investing is $0.20, and the benefit if at least one person invests is $1.00. If neither person in your group invests, both earn $0.20. Question 1: Suppose one person invests and the other person in your group does not. Then: a) the person who invests earns $0.80 and the other earns $1.00 b) the person who invests earns $1.00 and the other earns $0.80

Question 2: If both of you invest, you each earn (X); if neither of you invest, you each earn (Y). a) X = $1.00, Y = $0.20 b) X = $0.80, Y = $0.20

Matchings: At the beginning of each round, there will be a new random pairing of all participants, so the person who you are matched with in one round may not be the same person you will be matched with in the subsequent round. Matchings are random, and you are no more likely to be matched with one person than with another.

Earnings: All people will begin a round by choosing an investment decision, which entails a cost of $0.20. If at least one person in your group chooses to invest, both of you will receive a payment of $1.00, and your earnings will be this payment minus your investment cost (if any). If neither person invests, both receive $0.20.

Cumulative Earnings: Everybody begins with an initial cash balance of $0.00. Earnings will be added to this amount. The program will keep track of your cumulative earnings.

Rounds: There will be a number of rounds, with random rematchings at the beginning of each.

Special Earnings Announcement: Your cash earnings will be 50% of your total earnings at the end of the experiment.

19.1.2 Questions

  1. Write out the payoff matrix (in dollars) of the 2-player game in Parts 1 and 2. What are the players’ best responses to pure strategies? Are there any pure strategy Nash equilibria?

  2. For Game 1, let \(q\) be the probability that the column player invests. What \(q\) makes the row player indifferent between choosing to invest or not? Argue why this is a mixed strategy Nash equilibrium

  3. For Game 2:

    • Let \(p\) be the probability that the row player invests. What \(p\) makes the column player indifferent between investing and not investing?
    • Let \(q\) be the probability that column row player invests. What \(q\) makes the row player indifferent between investing and not investing?

  4. Fill Using your answers to the previous questions, fill in the blanks with one of “increases”, “decreases”, and “does not change”. Explain your answer:

When moving from Game 1 to Game 2, the probability that the row player invests ______, and the probability that the column player invests ______.

  1. Create a plot that shows the mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium predictions and the data from each round. Put round number on the horizontal axis, and predictions and data on the vertical axis.

  2. Compare your answer to question 4 to your plot in in question 5. How well does Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium do in predicting what happened? Evaluate this based on both point predictions and comparative static predictions. Does it do better for later rounds in each game? Explain any trends that you see.

    • (6260 students only) Do a formal hypothesis test of the the point and comparative static predictions. State your null and alternative.

  3. Think back to Principles of Microeconomics. Out of the four possible outcomes in each game, what combinations of row and column players’ actions maximize total surplus (i.e. total earnings)? Produce a plot that shows this theoretical maximum total earnings per round and actual earnings per round (vertical axis) for each round of the experiment (horizontal axis).

    • Did people on average get close to maximizing total surplus? If not, suggest why not.
    • Was is more or less difficult to maximize total surplus in Game 2 compared to Game 1? Explain your thinking behind this.

  4. For Game 1 only, suppose that players derive utility from their opponent earning money. That is, their payoff in utility, if they earn \(\$x\), and their opponent earns \(\$y\), is: \[ u(x,y)=x+\alpha y \]

    • (4260 students only) Assume that \(\alpha=0.5\), explain what these preferences mean in words, and derive predictions for Game 1 using this utility function.
    • (6260 students only) Estimate \(\alpha\) using data only from Game 1, explain what these preferences mean in words, and derive predictions for Games 1 and 2 using this utility function.

  5. (6260 students only) Suppose that instead of risk-neutral players, we had expected utility maximizers with utility function \(u(x)=x^r\). Using data from Game 1 only, estimate \(r\). Compare how this model, the model in question 8, and the risk-neutral model predicts play in Game 2.

This game is discussed in Section 9.3 of the text

19.2.1 Instructions

Rounds and Matchings: The experiment consists of a number of rounds. Note: In each round, you will be matched with another person selected at random from the other participants. There will be a new random rematching each round. The number of rounds is: 10.

Interdependence: Your earnings are determined by the decisions that you and the other person make. Each of you will be given the chance to make one or more decisions, in alternating order, until one person stops the process or until the final decision point is reached and the process is stopped automatically.

Roles: In each pair of people, one person will be selected at random to make the initial decision. If this person does not stop the process, then the other person will make the second decision. This person may stop the process, etc.

Payoffs: Press Continue to see the table of payoffs on the next page.

Your Decision: One person in each pair will be selected to make the first decision. This person will be called player A, and the two small circles or “radio buttons” on the left side of the above table correspond to the adjacent decision, Stop or Continue. If player A decides to Stop, then the payoffs will be $0.40 for Player A and $0.10 for Player B.

To Continue: At this time, suppose that you have the Player A role. Please mark a decision, Stop or Continue, and press the Submit Button below to go to the next page.

Since you selected Continue, Player B would see a table with radio buttons next to the two decisions, Continue or Stop, as shown below in the second column from the left. Now think of yourself as being in the role of Player B making the second decision, with your decisions and payoffs shown in the light blue font:

If you had selected Stop, Player B would have no decision to make and would see a table like the one below, where the payoffs are shown at the bottom left under the Stop decision. All of the payoffs in the subsequent columns are no longer relevant, and are shown in gray. The round would end at this point, and earnings would be added to your cumulative earnings.

Matchings: At the beginning of each round, there will be a new random pairing of all participants, so the person who you are matched with in one round may not be the same person you will be matched with in the subsequent round. Matchings are random, and you are no more likely to be matched with one person than with another.

Decisions: In each round, you and the person you are matched with will make Stop or Continue decisions in alternating order until one of you chooses to stop, or until the final Automatic Stop stage is reached.

Order of Decisions: The roles (Player A who moves first, or Player B who moves second) have been randomly determined, so your role, first or second mover, may change randomly from round to round.

Earnings: Your earnings are determined by the pair of payoffs in effect when the process is stopped. Earnings in each round will be added to your previous total. You will be paid your total earnings from all 20 rounds at the end of the final round.

Rounds: There will be 10 rounds, with random rematchings at the beginning of each.

Special Earnings Announcement: Your cash earnings will be 20% of your total earnings at the end of the experiment.

19.2.2 Questions

  1. Draw out the game trees for treatments 1 (rounds 1-10) and 2 (rounds 11-20). Make it clear which player is making the decision at each node.

  2. Determine the subgame-perfect Nash equilibrium for each game, assuming that players are selfish (i.e. they only care about their own monetary payoffs).

  3. For each treatment, plot the fraction of “Continue” decisions (vertical axis) for each node (horizontal axis).

  4. Comment on whether these fractions are at all in line with your predictions in question 2.

  5. Suppose that there is a fraction \(\theta\) of players who are willing to play “Continue” as long as their opponent has done so at all previous nodes. Let’s call this type of player a “conditionally cooperative” player.

    • (4260 students) How large does \(\theta\) have to be for a selfish player making a decision at the second to last node to be willing to play “Continue”?
    • (6260 students) How large does \(\theta\) have to be for a selfish player to be willing to play “Continue”? (i.e. the 4260 students’ question, but for every node). Note that this answer is a function of the node.

  6. Estimate this fraction using:

    • (4260 students) data from the final node of each game only.
    • (4260 students) data from the entire game.

  7. Suppose that conditionally cooperative players choose “Continue” with probability \(\rho>0.5\) (i.e. they make a mistake with probability \(1-\rho\)), and that selfish players choose their optimal action with probability \(\rho\) (their optimal action is a function of \(\rho\) and \(\theta\)). What could player A’s decision at the first node of Treatment 2 reveal that it may not reveal in Treatment 1? I am looking for qualitative answers only.

  8. (bonus question, not graded) Estimate \(\rho\) and \(\theta\) using the entire dataset.
